1. Introduction

Wine quality matters for producers, distributors, and consumers because it affects pricing and reputation. However, quality is often subjective and expensive to evaluate through expert tasting. This project investigates whether chemical measurements can be used to predict wine quality ratings.

Wine quality is influenced by many chemical characteristics, including acidity, sugar levels, sulphates, and alcohol content. These measurable properties play an important role in how wine is perceived by tasters and rated by experts.

This project uses the UCI Wine Quality dataset (red + white wines) to explore whether wine chemistry can predict:

  • Wine quality (Bad vs Good)

  • Wine type (Red vs White)

The analysis uses chemical laboratory measurements for both red and white wines. Wine quality scores originally range from 0–10 but are converted into a 100‑point scale for interpretability. The Wine Spectator scoring system was referenced to guide interpretation of the 100‑point scale: https://www.winespectator.com/articles/scoring-scale

The goal of this project is to explore whether wine chemistry can be used to predict wine outcomes. Specifically, this project investigates how chemical features relate to wine quality ratings and whether machine learning models can successfully classify wine quality. Additionally, this project examines whether red and white wines show distinct chemical patterns that allow accurate classification of wine type.

Research Questions:

  • How is wine quality distributed, and is it imbalanced?
  • Which chemical features are most associated with wine quality?
  • Can a Random Forest model classify wine as Bad vs Good?
  • Do red and white wines show distinct chemical patterns?
  • Can wine chemistry predict wine type accurately?

5. Supervised Learning: Predict Wine Quality (Bad vs Good)

The Wine Spectator scale considers wines scoring 80+ to be “Good,” but very few wines in this dataset reach that level. High‑quality wines are rare, which makes a three‑class or multi‑class prediction model unstable and highly imbalanced.

To reduce extreme imbalance and improve interpretability, wine quality was grouped into two categories:

  • Bad: quality < 70
  • Good: quality ≥ 70
## 
##  Bad Good 
## 5220 1277

This confirms that the dataset is imbalanced: Bad wines are the majority class, while Good wines are the minority class.

Train/Test Split

To evaluate the model fairly, the dataset was split into separate training and testing sets. Before splitting, the original quality variables (quality and quality_100) were removed to prevent data leakage, ensuring the model does not accidentally learn from the target values.

An 80/20 split was used:

  • 80% of the data was used to train the Random Forest model

  • 20% was held out for unbiased testing

Interestingly, wine_type had relatively low importance, meaning that red vs white classification alone does not strongly determine whether a wine is rated Good or Bad.

8. Conclusion

This project examined whether chemical measurements can be used to predict wine quality and wine type. Wine quality in the dataset was highly imbalanced, with most wines scoring between 50–70 points on the 100‑point scale and very few wines rated above 80, making high‑quality predictions more challenging.

The Random Forest model predicting wine quality (Bad vs Good) achieved strong overall accuracy of about 90%, but performance was weaker for Good wines due to class imbalance and limited high quality examples. Alcohol emerged as the most influential predictor of quality, followed by volatile acidity and density, reflecting well‑known relationships in wine chemistry.

The second Random Forest model, which classified wine type (red vs white), achieved exceptionally high accuracy (~99.5%), demonstrating that red and white wines have distinct chemical signatures. PCA further supported this result by showing clear separation between wine types using unsupervised dimensionality reduction.

Overall, the findings show that chemical measurements provide meaningful and reliable information for predicting wine outcomes. While wine type is easily distinguishable from chemistry alone, predicting higher‑quality wines remains difficult due to their scarcity in the dataset, highlighting the need for more balanced samples or alternative modeling strategies in future work.
