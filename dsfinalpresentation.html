Wine and its chemical properties

Brenda Nava

4/18/2026

Why study wine quality?

Wine quality matters for producers, distributors, and consumers because it affects pricing and reputation. However, quality is often subjective and expensive to evaluate through expert tasting. This project investigates whether chemical measurements can be used to predict wine quality ratings.

This project uses the UCI Wine Quality dataset (red + white wines) to explore whether wine chemistry can predict:

  • Wine quality (Bad vs Good)
  • Wine type (Red vs White)

Reference: Wine Spectator 100-point scale: https://www.winespectator.com/articles/scoring-scale

Research Questions:

  • How is wine quality distributed, and is it imbalanced?
  • Which chemical features are most associated with wine quality?
  • Can a Random Forest model classify wine as Bad vs Good?
  • Do red and white wines show distinct chemical patterns?
  • Can wine chemistry predict wine type accurately?

This project uses exploratory data analysis (EDA) to understand chemical relationships and quality distribution. Then, supervised learning (Random Forest classification) is applied to predict wine quality categories. Finally, unsupervised learning (PCA) is used to examine whether red and white wines naturally form separate chemical clusters.

Dataset Description

  • UCI Wine Quality Dataset
  • Red wine: 1,599 rows
  • White wine: 4,898 rows
  • Combined: 6,497 rows
  • 11 chemical variables
  • Added wine_type label
  • Created quality score: 0–10 (converted to 100-point scale) score for interpretability.
  • No missing values

Features: fixed acidity, volatile acidity, citric acid, residual sugar, chlorides, sulphates, alcohol, sulfur dioxide, density and pH. Limitations: No grape variety, brand, or vintage year.

Supervised Random Forest Model 1: Predict wine quality (Bad vs Good)

Why Binary Categories?

The Wine Spectator scale suggests 80+ is “Good”, but very few wines fall into that range. High quality wines are rare in this dataset, so 3-class prediction was unstable.

To avoid extreme imbalance, quality was grouped into::

  • Bad: quality < 70
  • Good: quality ≥ 70

The model struggles more with “Good” wines because they are the minority class.

Accuracy is high (~90%):

  • Model predicts Bad wines extremely well
  • Model struggles more predicting Good wines due to imbalance

  • Mean Decrease Accuracy: Drop in accuracy if variable is disturbed; important for prediction.

  • Mean Decrease Gini: How much it improves tree splits; separates classes.

Alcohol was the strongest predictor of wine quality, followed by volatile acidity and density. This suggests that wine quality ratings are influenced strongly by alcohol content and the balance between acidity and density. Interestingly, wine_type had relatively low importance, meaning that red vs white classification alone does not strongly determine whether a wine is rated Good or Bad.

Supervised Random Forest Model 2: Predict wine type (red vs white)

Now we test whether chemical features can predict wine type.

Wine type was predicted with ~99.5% accuracy, showing red and white wines have distinct chemical profiles.

Variable importance for wine type

The most important features for distinguishing red and white wines include total sulfur dioxide, chlorides, and volatile acidity. These results align with real-world expectations, since white wines tend to contain higher sugar and sulfur dioxide levels compared to red wines.

Unsupervised Model using Principal Component Analysis (PCA)

  • PCA was performed without wine_type labels
  • Red and white wines still formed separate clusters

Wine type separation is naturally present in chemistry data.

What PC1 and PC2 mean: [These new components are weighted mixtures of the original variables]

  • PC1 captures the largest amount of variation in the dataset.
  • PC2 captures the second largest amount of variation, and is independent of PC1.

The PCA visualization shows clear separation between red and white wines in principal component space. This indicates that wine type differences are strongly reflected in chemical measurements, even without using wine type labels in the PCA calculation. This supports the supervised learning result that wine chemistry can reliably distinguish wine type.

Conclusion

  • Wine quality is highly imbalanced (most wines between 50–70)
  • Good wines were harder to classify due to fewer samples
  • Alcohol, volatile acidity, and density are the strongest predictors.
  • Random Forest predicted Bad vs Good with ~90% accuracy
  • Red and white wines show distinct chemical subgroupings.
  • Wine type (red vs white) was predicted extremely well (~99.5%)
  • PCA confirmed strong separation between wine types

Insights:

  • Chemistry can be used as a low cost tool to estimate quality
  • Wine type classification suggests strong chemical signatures

Limitations:

  • High-quality wines are rare = imbalance bias
  • 100-point scale is simplified (quality*10 is not perfect)
